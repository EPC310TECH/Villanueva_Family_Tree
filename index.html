About this project

What is the Villanueva Family Tree?

This is an interactive 3D genealogy visualization tracing the ancestry of the Villanueva / Jasso family — rooted in present-day Mexico — backwards through centuries of Iberian nobility, medieval kingdoms, and early medieval Europe.

Each node in the graph is a real documented person. Edges represent parent–child bonds. The further up you navigate, the further back in time — stretching all the way to the 4th century AD.

The data was sourced from Geni.com and processed through a custom Python pipeline that parses, deduplicates, classifies, and renders everything into a single force-directed graph.

Nodes are sized by pedigree collapse — how many separate lineage paths converge on that one ancestor. The most important nodes in the medieval period appear in thousands of convergent lines simultaneously. Nodes are colored by historical role: royal, noble, clergy, military, and so on.

Data analysis

What the data reveals

Patterns hidden inside the genealogy — surfaced by analyzing node connectivity, birth years, geographic distribution, and pedigree collapse.

× 4,758 Highest pedigree collapse

Fernán González, Count of Castile (b. 910) appears across 4,758 separate lineage paths in this tree — meaning he is a mathematical common ancestor converging through thousands of distinct family branches simultaneously.

His consort Sancha Sánchez de Navarra, Queen Consort of León shares the same figure. Together they represent the single most significant bottleneck in the entire dataset — every branch of this family traces back through them.

Gor Thorrasson, Sea King — born in the 4th century — is the earliest known person in the tree at generation 16. He appears in the lineage through the Norse royal lines that eventually merged with Frankish and then Iberian nobility over the following centuries.

Other early ancestors include Chindasvinto, King of the Visigoths (b. 563) and Miro, King of the Suevi (b. 550) — kings of post-Roman Iberia.

Pedigree collapse is a known mathematical phenomenon in genealogy: the further back you go, the more your ancestors must "share" people, since the number of slots (2, 4, 8, 16…) grows faster than the population of the time. In this tree it's dramatic — a handful of 9th–10th century Navarrese and Castilian counts appear in the ancestry of virtually every later Iberian royal house.

225 Documented monarchs

The tree passes through 225 kings, queens, and sovereign rulers — from Norse sea kings in the 4th century to the monarchs of medieval Castile, León, Navarre, Aragon, France, England, Norway, Portugal, and beyond.

That includes rulers of kingdoms that no longer exist: the Visigoths, the Suevi, Caucasian Albania, the Franks, and Asturias — the small Christian kingdom in northern Spain from which the eventual reconquest of Iberia began.

3 Indigenous Huachichil ancestors

The dataset's indigenous roots trace to the Huachichil — a Chichimec people of north-central Mexico — through three documented ancestors:

María India Huachichil (b. 1527) and an unnamed Huachichil ancestor are the earliest. Their descendant Miguel Caldera (b. 1548) is one of the most remarkable figures in the tree: a mestizo military captain whose Spanish father and Huachichil mother positioned him as a cultural bridge between the two worlds. He negotiated the peace that ended the Chichimec War in 1590 — the same conflict Baltasar Temiño y Bañuelos fought in — making him arguably the most historically significant person in the Mexican branch of this lineage.

40 Mexican colonial ancestors

The tree documents 40 ancestors placed in Mexico — the colonial-era bridge between the Iberian lineages and the present-day Villanueva / Jasso family.

Among the most recent documented ancestors: Maria Soledad Bañuelos de la Torre (b. 1788) and Blasa Bartola Cipriana de la Torre y de Aro (b. 1746) — families of Spanish colonial descent settled in central Mexico.

4 Soldiers of the Spanish conquest

Among your documented ancestors are soldiers from the Spanish conquest era — men who crossed the Atlantic and took part in the military campaigns that ended the Aztec Empire and opened the Mexican frontier.

Confirmed · Cortés captain
Conquistador y Capitán Luis Marín b. 1490 · d. 1547

One of Hernán Cortés's most trusted officers during the 1519–1521 conquest of the Aztec Empire. After Tenochtitlán fell, Cortés personally sent him to conquer Chiapas in 1524. His daughter Marina Mendoza de Marín carries his line into the family tree.

Likely · same generation
Pedro de Bobadilla d. ~1550

Titled "Conquistador de Nueva España" — the formal designation given to veterans of the Cortés campaign. His death date (~1550) places him squarely in the conquest generation. Birth year is not recorded in the dataset.

Next generation · Nueva Galicia
Francisco de Bobadilla b. 1515 · d. 1570

"Conquistador de la Nueva Galicia" — part of the 1530s–40s Spanish push into the northwest frontier (modern Jalisco and Zacatecas). Born too young for Cortés's original campaign, but part of the same colonial wave that followed it.

Later generation · Chichimec War
Capitán General Baltasar Temiño y Bañuelos b. 1530 · d. 1600

Fought in the Chichimec War — the prolonged guerrilla conflict across north-central Mexico (1550–1590). His campaigns helped secure the silver-road from Zacatecas to Mexico City that funded the Spanish Empire for two centuries.

22 Documented generations

The deepest branch reaches generation 22 — about 22 parent–child steps back from the living root. At roughly 25–30 years per generation that implies ancestry stretching to the 400s–500s, which aligns with the earliest documented people in the dataset.

Of 4,554 ancestors, 2,962 have a documented birth year — about 65% of the total. The remaining 35% are known from genealogical records but lack confirmed dates.

Active Research

This tree is still growing

The Villanueva Family Tree is an ongoing research project. New ancestor data is being added regularly as more source files are processed and new branches are traced through Geni.com and archival records.

Every new addition is automatically merged into the master graph, with deduplication ensuring that shared ancestors across branches are counted only once and their pedigree weight accumulates correctly.
