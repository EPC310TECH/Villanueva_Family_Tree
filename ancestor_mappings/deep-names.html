Generation numbers count parent-to-child steps back from Antonio Jasso (gen 0). "Converging lines" is the number of separate paths in the pedigree that funnel through that person — a measure of how structurally central they are to the tree, not how much genetic material they contributed.

Names are listed within their layer and sorted by the oldest attested ancestor who carried them. The etymology traces the original language and word-stems, not later folk-etymology.

A note on the deepest layer: the Kvenland kings (Fornjót, Kári, Snær, Thorri, Gorr) are legendary, not historical. They come from Norse sagas — the Orkneyinga saga and Hversu Noregr byggðist — which trace Norwegian dynasties back to personified forces of nature. They are genuine parts of the recorded genealogy, but they belong to myth. Everyone from roughly 700 AD forward is historical.

I The Norse & Legendary Layer gen 34 – 47

Old Norse names drawn from forces of nature, battle, and the divine. The earliest entries are legendary — mythological personifications recorded in the sagas. From Halfdan onward the names become historical.

ThorriLegendary
Old Norse Þorri — the name of the fourth winter month in the old Icelandic calendar, still marked today by the midwinter feast Þorrablót. Part of a family of snow-words; not a reference to the god Thor.
  • King Thorri Snærsson of Kvenland
    fl. ~310 · gen 47 · Legendary
    In the sagas his children are Nór, Gorr, and Gói. The disappearance of his daughter Gói sets the founding myth of Norway in motion.
    34converging lines
SnærLegendary
Old Norse Snær — literally "snow." His children are named for kinds of snowfall: Þorri, Fönn, Drífa, and Mjöll.
  • King Snø "the Old" Karasson of Kvenland
    Legendary · gen 46
    His children are all snow: Þorri, Fönn, Drífa, and Mjöll — all Old Norse words for kinds of snowfall.
    24converging lines
FrostiLegendary
Old Norse Frosti — "frost." Also recorded as a dwarf-name in the Poetic Edda.
  • Frosti Kárasson, Mythical King of Kvenland
    Legendary · gen 45
    Legendary son of Kári the wind.
    12converging lines
KáriLegendary
Old Norse Kári — in ordinary use, "curly-haired." In the Fornjót myth, the personification of wind. Son of Fornjót, brother to Logi (fire) and Hlér/Ægir (sea): wind, fire, and sea.
  • King Kári "the Wind" Fornjótsson of Kvenland
    Legendary · gen 44
    The three brothers are wind, fire, and sea — the elemental forces behind the genealogical myth of Scandinavia.
    6converging lines
GorrLegendary
Old Norse Górr — meaning debated. In the sagas, Gorr took the sea while his brother Nór took the land: Nór's line became the kings of Norway, Gorr's the sea kings and earls of Orkney.
  • Gorr Thorrasson, Sea King
    365 – 418 · gen 38 · Oldest documented person in the tree
    The oldest documented person in the entire tree, at generation 38.
    39converging lines
RaumHistorical
Old Norse Raumr — probably "large man." The name behind the district Raumaríki (modern Romerike, Norway). Alfheim was the mythic land of the elves.
  • Raum "the Old" Norsson, King of Alfheim
    370 – 450 · gen 37
    Son of Nór, placed in the mythic Alfheim between the Glomma and Göta rivers.
HildurHistorical
Old Norse Hildr — "battle." A valkyrie name, and the root inside dozens of Germanic names: Brunhild, Mathilde, Richilde.
  • Hildur Gudraudsdatter
    371 – 470 · gen 36
    One of the two oldest persons in the tree alongside Gorr.
HalfdanHistorical
Old Norse Hálfdan — "half-Dane." Originally descriptive: one Danish parent. Recycled relentlessly across Norse dynasties.
  • Halfdan "the Tall" Frodasson, King of Denmark
    503 – 580
  • Halfdan the Valiant
    590 – 650
  • Halfdan "Gamle" Sveidasson
    "Halfdan the Old."
IvarHistorical
Old Norse Ívarr — most likely ýr ("yew," the wood of bows) + herr ("warrior") — "bow-warrior."
  • King Ivar "Vidfamne" Halfdansson
    612 – 647
    "Ivar the Wide-Reaching" — a legendary Swedish king said to have ruled much of Scandinavia and beyond.
  • Ivar Halfdansson, Opplendingejarl
    760 – 824 · Earl of the Uplands, Norway
    44converging lines
EysteinHistorical
Old Norse Eysteinn — ey ("luck," or "island") + steinn ("stone") — "lucky stone." His byname Glumra means "the noisy" or "the clamorous."
  • Eystein Ivarsson "the Noisy" Glumra
    810 – 870 · Norwegian chieftain, father of Ragnvald
    53converging lines
RagnvaldHistorical
Old Norse Rögnvaldr — regin ("the gods, counsel") + valdr ("ruler") — "ruler by the counsel of the gods." The same name, root for root, as English Ronald.
  • Ragnvald Eysteinsson, Earl of Møre
    825 – 894
    One of Harald Fairhair's closest allies in the unification of Norway; granted Orkney and Shetland. Father of Rollo.
    89converging lines
RagnarHistorical
Old Norse Ragnarr — regin ("the gods") + herr ("army") — "warrior of the gods."
  • Ragnar "Lodbrok" Sigurdsson, King of Denmark and Norway
    749 – 845
    The legendary Viking of the sagas. Loðbrók means "hairy-breeches."
SigurdHistorical
Old Norse Sigurðr — sigr ("victory") + vǫrðr ("guardian") — "victory's guardian." The dragon-slayer of the Völsunga saga; Wagner's Siegfried.
  • Sigurd "Ring" Randversson
    724 – 812 · Semi-legendary Danish king, father of Ragnar Lodbrok
Rollo · HrólfrHistorical
Old Norse Hrólfr, contracted from Hróðulfr — hróðr ("fame") + úlfr ("wolf") — "famous wolf." Latinized by Frankish scribes as Rollo. His byname Gange means "the Walker" — he was said to be too heavy for any horse.
  • Gange-Hrólfr "Rollo" Ragnvaldsson
    d. 928
    Besieged Paris, took Normandy from Charles the Simple in 911, and founded the ducal line that produced William the Conqueror.
    665converging lines — one of the great bottlenecks of the entire tree
GunnorHistorical
Old Norse Gunnvǫr — gunnr ("war") + vǫr ("vigilant") — "war-watchful." Note: the modern name Nora is a phonetic echo, not a true descendant — Nora comes from Honoria/Eleanora.
  • Gunnor, Duchess of Normandy
    936 – 1031
    Danish noblewoman, wife of Richard I of Normandy. Formidably long-lived and politically central; her kin network staffed the Norman aristocracy for generations.
    628converging lines
PoppaHistorical
Frankish, uncertain — probably from the Germanic name-stem Popo / Boppo. She and Rollo are the same bottleneck in the pedigree.
  • Poppa of Bayeux
    872 – 930
    Rollo's wife by Norse custom (more danico), mother of William Longsword. Reportedly the captured daughter of a Frankish count.
    665converging lines
RurikHistorical
Old Norse Hrøríkr — hróðr ("fame") + ríkr ("ruler") — "famous ruler." The same name, root for root, as Gothic-Spanish Rodrigo — which appears 41 times elsewhere in this tree.
  • Rurik, Grand Duke of Novgorod
    830 – 879
    The Varangian founder of the Rurikid dynasty, which ruled Russia for seven centuries until 1598.
IgorHistorical
Russian form of Old Norse Ingvarr — Yngvi (a name of the god Freyr) + herr ("warrior") — "Ing's warrior."
  • Igor "the Old" Rurikov of Kiev
    877 – 945 · Grand Prince of Kiev, son of Rurik
    Killed by the Drevlians while collecting tribute twice in one season; his widow Olga's revenge is one of the set-pieces of the Primary Chronicle.
    63converging lines
II The Visigothic & Asturian Layer gen 34 – 43

The Visigoths ruled Spain from the 5th century until the Islamic conquest of 711. Their names are Gothic — a Germanic language — which is why they look nothing like later Spanish. The Asturian layer immediately follows: the generation that survived the conquest and began the Reconquista.

ChindasvintoHistorical
Gothic — the second element is swinþs = "strong." A signature Visigothic name-ending shared with Recceswinth, Sisenand, and others.
  • Chindasvinth, King of the Visigoths
    563 – 653
    Seized the throne at roughly 79 years old, purged the nobility that had made kings disposable, and promulgated the Liber Iudiciorum, the law code that governed Spain for centuries.
    26converging lines
Glasvinta · GlasuindaHistorical
Gothic — same swinþs ("strong") ending in its feminine form. Compare the Visigothic queens Goiswintha and Brunhilda.
  • Princess Flavia Glasuinda Tavira dos Visigodos
    b. 620 · Visigothic princess
    40converging lines
  • Flavia Glasvinta
    b. 590
  • Glasvinta Ferrara
    b. 645
FavilaHistorical
Visigothic, uncertain. A Visigothic nobleman of the generation that lost Spain in 711 — the hinge moment of the family's history.
  • Favila, Duke of Cantabria
    Father of Pelayo. His generation watched the world they inherited dissolve in a single summer.
    10converging lines
PelayoHistorical
From Latin Pelagius, from Greek pélagos = "the open sea" — "the man of the sea."
  • Pelayo, King of Asturias
    685 – 737
    The founder of Christian Spain. After the Muslim conquest he withdrew into the Asturian mountains and won the battle of Covadonga (~722), traditionally counted as the first act of the Reconquista — the 770-year process that eventually produced the Castile your family descends from.
    12converging lines
GaudiosaHistorical
From Latin gaudium = "joy" — "the joyful one."
  • Gaudiosa
    675 – 737 · Queen of Asturias, wife of Pelayo
    She and Pelayo are recorded with the same year of death.
    12converging lines
FruelaHistorical
Visigothic Froila, from Gothic frauja = "lord" — roughly "little lord." A satisfying cognate: Gothic frauja ("lord") and Old Norse freyja ("lady," hence the goddess Freya) descend from the same Proto-Germanic root. Fruela and Freya are distant linguistic relatives.
  • Fruela, Duke of Cantabria
    710 – 765
    224converging lines
  • Fruela Bermúdez de Trava, Count of Trastámara
    911 – 997
HereswinthaHistorical
Germanic — heri ("army") + swinþs ("strong") — "army-strong." The Visigothic strength-ending, applied here to a Saxon noblewoman.
  • Hereswintha of the Saxons
    700 – 747
    372converging lines — a major junction of the tree
III The Frankish & Carolingian Layer gen 28 – 39

Most of these are built from a small set of Germanic word-stems, recombined endlessly: ermen (universal), ric (ruler), hild (battle), gard (protection), thrud (strength), adal (noble). The creativity was not in invention but in combination.

Germanic — ermen ("whole, universal") + gard ("enclosure, protection") — "universal protection."

    691 – 751

IV The Iberian Layer gen 20 – 33

The closest layer — names from the Iberian peninsula, a mix of Basque, Latin, and older pre-Roman roots that defied the Germanic wave. This is where the family's deepest pedigree collapses: Toda Aznárez at 3,582 lines is the single largest bottleneck in the entire tree.

UrracaHistorical
Iberian — from Spanish urraca = "magpie." Almost certainly a nickname that hardened into a royal name; some argue a Basque origin. The single greatest point of pedigree collapse in the entire tree.
  • Urraca I, Queen of Castile and León
    1080 – 1126
    The first woman to reign as queen regnant in her own right anywhere in Western Europe. The ancestor on whom both of the family's independent royal descents converge.
    252converging lines — greatest single collapse in the tree
  • Urraca de Castilla, Queen Consort of Asturias
    b. 780
TodaHistorical
Basque / Navarrese, uncertain — possibly from Latin tota ("all"), possibly native Basque. The formidable matriarch of Navarre; grandmother, aunt, or cousin to nearly every Iberian monarch of her century.
  • Toda Aznárez de Larraún, Queen of Navarre
    876 – 958
    A working diplomat between the Christian kingdoms and the Caliphate of Córdoba. She personally led a diplomatic mission to Córdoba to secure medical treatment for her grandson from the caliph's physician.
    3,582converging lines — the deepest bottleneck in the entire pedigree

Occitan / Old French, uncertain. Famous for what happened next: when Aénor of Châtellerault's daughter was born, she was called alia Aenor — "the other Aenor" — which contracted into Eleanor.

A closing note on weight

Every person in this document is a real, documented ancestor. But it's worth being honest about scale: an ancestor at generation 38 contributes something on the order of one part in 250 billion of anyone's actual ancestry. Gorr the Sea King is a magnificent story and a genuine line in the record. He is not, in any measurable sense, why anyone in this family looks the way they do.

The "converging lines" counts are the better measure of importance here. They show how central a person is to the structure of the pedigree — how many separate roads run through them. Toda Aznárez at 3,582 and Rollo at 665 are load-bearing. Gorr, at 39, is the end of a long and beautiful thread.

The legendary layer (everything before ~700 AD) records how medieval Scandinavians understood their own origins — a genealogy that begins with wind, snow, and sea. Whether or not Fornjót existed, the fact that Norwegian chieftains traced themselves to him tells you something real about how they thought. That is its own kind of history.
